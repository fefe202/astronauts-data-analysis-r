# Course Project Description

## 2026 - Introduction to Data Analysis with R

As part of the course, you will complete a group data analysis project based on a real dataset. The project is intended to help you apply the methods and tools discussed during the semester in a practical and structured way.

The project should be completed in groups of four students. At the end of the semester, each group will present and defend its project during class.

## Dataset

We will be using data from the TidyTuesday initiative, which offers a wide range of interesting, well-structured, and publicly available datasets. Each group should choose its dataset by the end of March and enter the dataset name in the shared Excel file on Teams.

## General Requirements

The entire project must be prepared in R Markdown (.Rmd).
Your submission should not only contain code, but also a clear description of all important steps, the reasoning behind your choices, and the interpretation of results. This is not just a coding task — it is a full analytical report.

## Scope of Work

### 1. Data Cleaning and Preparation

Begin by loading the data and carefully examining its structure.
Your work in this part should include:
checking variables, data types, and missing values,
identifying and correcting errors or inconsistencies,
handling missing observations appropriately,
transforming the data into a format suitable for further analysis.
The aim of this stage is to prepare a clean and understandable dataset that can be used for exploration and modeling.

### 2. Exploratory Data Analysis (EDA)

In this part, you should explore the dataset in detail and try to understand its main patterns and relationships.
This stage should include:
a description of the variables,
basic descriptive statistics,
identification of interesting trends, structures, and possible relationships,
creation or transformation of variables.
Each group must create at least 10 new columns in total. These may include, for example:
recoded variables,
grouped categories,
calculated indicators,
transformations,
aggregated values.
In addition, the project must contain at least 10 different charts showing the most important aspects of the data.
For each chart, provide a short explanation:
why you created it,
what it shows,
how it helps us better understand the dataset.
The focus should be on meaningful and well-interpreted visualizations, not only on technical correctness.

### 3. Modeling

This part should go beyond description and attempt to answer one or more analytical questions using statistical models.
Start by formulating at least one research hypothesis or analytical question, for example:
Is there a significant relationship between two variables?
Can one variable be predicted based on others?
Which factors seem to matter most for a selected outcome?
Then build at least two different models using methods discussed during the course.
Possible examples include:
linear regression,
logistic regression,
or other relevant methods introduced in class.
For each model, explain:
why you selected the dependent and independent variables,
how the model works,
how you interpret the coefficients or main parameters,
how you evaluate the model fit or quality,
what conclusions can be drawn from the results.
You should also compare the models, for example in terms of:
interpretability,
goodness of fit,
statistical significance,
prediction quality,
practical usefulness.

### 4. Summary and Reflection

The final part of the report should summarize the entire project and reflect on the analytical process.
Please include:
a concise summary of what was done,
the most important findings,
the main conclusions,
a discussion of difficulties or limitations,
reflection on what worked well and what could be improved.
You have to also address questions such as:
What was the most difficult part of the project?
What was the most interesting part?
What would you do differently if you started again?
What would you improve if you had more time, more knowledge, or more resources?
This section is important because it shows your understanding of the whole process, not only the final output.

## Project Organization

### Group Work

The project must be completed in teams of four. Please organize your groups and sign up in the shared Excel file on Teams.

### Consultations

Project sessions will have a consultative format. This means there will be no mandatory regular project meetings, but I will monitor progress from time to time and help when needed.

Presentation and Defense

During the last classes of the semester, each group will give a short presentation of its project and answer questions about it.
The presentation should include:
a short introduction to the dataset,
the main stages of the analysis,
the most important visualizations,
the models used,
key findings and conclusions.
All group members should be prepared to answer questions about the project. The defense is meant to confirm that the group understands the work it has submitted.

## Submission Format

The final submission must be provided as an .Rmd file.
Make sure the file includes:
code,
comments,
explanations,
interpretations,
charts,
models,
and the final summary.
Your report should be readable, logically structured, and professionally prepared.

What Will Be Especially Important
Even technically correct code is not enough on its own. The quality of your explanations, analytical thinking, and interpretation will be an important part of the evaluation.

Final Note
Choose a dataset that your group finds genuinely interesting — this usually leads to better questions, better analysis, and better results.
